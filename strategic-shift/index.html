Strategic Reset

Why we're shifting focus away from the NHS as our primary market.

Three years of structured NHS engagement have built genuine domain expertise, a robust evidence base, and a verified business case. They have not, however, translated into scaled adoption. This is the honest account of what we learned, why the NHS market is structurally harder than it looks - and where Lutra is heading next.

The reframing

This is a shift in focus - not a step away.

We have invested significant time in the NHS over the past three years. The conclusion isn't that the NHS doesn't matter - it's that it can't be the primary engine of our growth at our current stage. New NHS opportunities will continue to be welcomed and supported. They will not, however, define the roadmap.

01 / The structural reality

Six structural barriers that make NHS adoption disproportionately hard.

Each of these is solvable in isolation. Stacked together, they have absorbed more time, capital and roadmap concessions than the resulting traction can justify for a company at our stage.

01

Multi-stakeholder buy-in is required, with misaligned incentives.

Pathway change with Lutra isn't a sale to a Trust, an ICB, or an LOC - it requires all three aligned simultaneously. ICBs are reeling under redundancies and budget cuts, and the underlying incentives don't agree: Trusts want additional revenue from ICBs; ICBs are trying to reduce it. Devon case

Coordination cost
02

Point solutions own the political zeitgeist - and the budget.

AI scribes can sell directly to a Trust on a simple ROI with far fewer stakeholders. They are also where the political attention sits - meaning the available NHS digital funding is being siphoned toward AI-related suppliers. Lutra is not in that zeitgeist.

Budget gravity
03

NHS-wide budget cuts blunt even a verified business case.

Our business case has been independently verified by NHS Business Intelligence at greater than 8× ROI. Even with that, traction has been hard to manufacture against the prevailing financial headwind. Appendix A

Macro environment
04

The SPoA category is fragmented, entrenched and fatigued.

Some ICBs have adopted lighter referral systems (e.g. EyeV) as their Single Point of Access. Others, like Devon, have niche local SPoAs - the Devon Referral Support Service (DRSS) - which are deeply embedded and require integration. CrossCover at Moorfields, Cinapsis and EyeV occupy the rest. Devon case Competitors

Even where Lutra is demonstrably superior, considerable political will has gone into adopting the existing system. Appetite for another big change is minimal.

Change fatigue
05

The tender process favours scale, not the firms doing the hard yards.

Even with full stakeholder buy-in, contracts are awarded through tender - where competitors with more existing NHS traction and the relevant compliance certification (ISO 27001, ISO 9001) are structurally advantaged. NHS Wales is the case in point: we did the foundational work; the contract went elsewhere.

Procurement risk
06

EPR rollouts can override every other signal.

NHS Somerset is the cleanest illustration: clear ROI, ophthalmology buy-in, CEO support - all overridden by a Digital team refusing to entertain new digital projects until Epic is fully embedded. That timeline is realistically 2+ years. Appendix C

Programme freeze
8×ROI

Verified outcomes are the foundation, not the finish line.

NHS Business Intelligence has independently verified Lutra's return on investment at greater than 8×, and in Tayside that has translated directly into outcomes. Scaling beyond Tayside depends on alignment with national programmes, ICB digital priorities, and EPR rollouts already in flight: timelines that exist for legitimate reasons and run independently of any individual product's value case. Our shift in focus is about building additional adoption channels alongside continued NHS engagement.

02 / What the nations taught us

Four nations, four versions of the same lesson.

Each devolved health system imposed its own form of structural lock-in. Together they describe the shape of the wider problem: even where there's genuine clinical interest, system-level commitments elsewhere keep the door closed.

England

Fragmentation by design.

High effort · Low conversion

Primary Eyecare Services (PES) sits at the centre of community eye-care commissioning - a pivotal player that's structurally hard to bypass. Beneath that, NHS England is fundamentally fragmented: each ICB operates differently.

Many have already adopted PES (and its associated digital system for managing optometry referrals). Others run on Cinapsis or EyeV. Either way, the addressable market shrinks ICB by ICB before we get into the room.

Posture: opportunistic, not resource-led Devon case
Wales

Tender process; re-entry planned.

Re-entering in < 6 months

The first Wales national contract did not land for us - and the process taught us a great deal about ISO requirements and NHS procurement mechanics. The tender is expected to kick off again in the next six months and we will apply.

Wales remains a real opportunity, but it's a tender-shaped one - won by NHS-track-record and certification posture, not by demonstrated outcomes alone.

Posture: re-apply, with sharper compliance positioning
Scotland

National programme commitments in place.

Programme-locked

The NHS Scotland Innovation Team was supportive. The constraint sat with NHS Scotland's Centre for Sustainable Delivery - specifically the Ophthalmic Workforce - for two reasons.

First, current policy is to direct optometry referrals through SCI Gateway. Second, NHS Scotland is actively driving adoption of OpenEyes, procured pre-COVID for Scotland-wide rollout under the Once-for-Scotland EPR programme. With those national commitments in place, our Tayside outcomes data was not sufficient on its own to open a parallel route.

Posture: high-effort, low-return; deprioritise Engagement record
Northern Ireland

Recent system changes already in flight.

Change-capacity constrained

We engaged the lead ophthalmologist and the eye-care delivery lead - the right people, genuinely interested. Northern Ireland has recently adopted SCI Gateway for optometry referrals and Epic as the hospital EPR, with the integration now live.

Despite Lutra's lower cost and broader benefits, the lead ophthalmologist did not want to introduce further system change to her clinical and admin teams during the current transition. Available change capacity, rather than the merits of the product, was the deciding factor.

Posture: hold; revisit when the dust settles

Strong outcomes, on their own, have not been sufficient to unlock scaled adoption.

The signed agreement (Feb 2025)

A formal commercial partnership was negotiated and signed between Lutra Health and Royal Devon University Healthcare NHS Foundation Trust, with Dave Tarbet executing on the Trust side, on the recommendation of Roger Killen (Lutra Investor Director).

  • Royalty cap of £100k on Lutra revenue from non-RDUH NHS England Trusts/ICBs (5% of gross), over a 5-year period beginning post-pilot.
  • IP retained by Lutra; outstanding historical fees of £8k cleared in two tranches.
  • Two pilots agreed in principle: Nightingale (post-op + PROMs/PREMs, funded from existing £67k NHSE grant) and North Devon (Advice & Guidance).

The agreement was the foundational commercial step: Lutra would partner with RDUH to demonstrate outcomes in Devon, and the Trust would benefit (via royalties) as Lutra scaled across other NHS England Trusts as a result.

What has actually launched (Apr 2026)

Nothing. Despite the signed agreement, four stalled workstreams, and active clinical advocates, no Lutra deployment is live in NHS Devon - either at RDUH, Nightingale, or under the ICB. The £67k NHSE grant still sits in the RDUH account, unused.

Each workstream has been deferred, paused, or absorbed into another stakeholder's process at least once. None has produced a launched pilot.

Beneath all of this: integration with DRSS

None of the email correspondence above explicitly engages with the operational integration layer - but it is sitting underneath every workstream. Devon is one of the ICBs covered in section 01 (Wall 04) as having a niche local Single Point of Access: the Devon Referral Support Service (DRSS).

What this evidence demonstrates

A signed commercial agreement is not, on its own, an operational route.

Devon is the strongest illustration in this brief of why multi-stakeholder coordination cost (Wall 01) is not a theoretical concern but the dominant operational reality. Trust, Nightingale, ICB, HRC, and the embedded DRSS layer each sit on independent timelines - and progress on any one is regularly absorbed by changes on another. After fourteen months, with a signed partnership agreement, an unspent £67k NHSE grant, an 8× verified ROI from Tayside, clinical advocacy across multiple Trust departments, and an academic evaluation arm willing to support, no project has launched. The bottleneck has never been product fit. It has been the structure of the buyer.

We considered proposing an equity stake in Lutra to PES, but they had already begun building their own platform internally, so we decided against pursuing it.

Why PES matters strategically

PES is the leading provider of NHS-commissioned community eye services in England. The platform PES adopts effectively becomes the de facto optometry-to-ophthalmology system across a substantial slice of the country.

  • PES previously contracted Opera as its digital backbone - now sunsetting and due to be replaced.
  • Replacing Opera was the natural commercial opening for Lutra; we engaged PES on exactly that basis through 2025.
  • PES has chosen to build its own replacement internally rather than procure from an external supplier.
  • PES's most active regions (Manchester, Merseyside & Cheshire) overlap with the highest NHS ophthalmology waiting lists in England - the territory where downstream impact would be largest.

What PES is building

According to the engagement record (December 2025), PES has hired an engineering team / consultancy to build out a core technology stack covering:

  • Single patient record - the same foundational layer Lutra has been operating in production.
  • Simple forms for patient communications.
  • Business intelligence and reporting.
  • Image sharing.
  • Strong stated interest in agentic AI, automated screening & triage, and voice interfaces as future direction.
  • Integration treated as outsourced; specific delivery partners not disclosed.

The stack PES has set out to build is materially the same stack Lutra has already deployed in production at NHS Tayside.

What this evidence demonstrates

The single largest NHS England optometry distribution channel has chosen to build, not buy.

PES is not a product-fit blocker, it is a structural one. Their decision to build internally closes off the most material commercial path into NHS England community optometry referrals. We considered an equity-led partnership but, given PES's internal-build trajectory, decided not to pursue it. This is the canonical example of a competitor outcome that no amount of product improvement on Lutra's side could have changed, and is a primary reason the strategic shift toward optometry-led pull and private ophthalmology is necessary.

Cinapsis & EyeV - incumbent SPoA platforms holding ICB territory.

A second category of competitor sits one layer below PES: the incumbent Single-Point-of-Access platforms operating across NHS England. Where PES closes off market access through its commissioning role, Cinapsis and EyeV close it off ICB by ICB through embedded operational deployments. Replacing them at any given ICB requires displacing a system in active use - the “change fatigue” dynamic described in Wall 04 of the overview.

Cinapsis

Profile. Multi-specialty advice & guidance and clinical-messaging platform. Used across primary care for secure photo-sharing referrals, A&G messaging, and triage routing - not eye-care-specific, which is both a strength (broader installed base) and a constraint (less optimised for ophthalmic workflow).

Footprint. Deployed across multiple NHS England ICBs as the primary A&G and referral-messaging tool, including in regions where ophthalmology shares the platform with dermatology, ENT and other specialties. The company is well-funded and has been a consistent winner of multi-specialty A&G procurements.

Implication for Lutra. In any ICB where Cinapsis is the default A&G layer, displacing it for ophthalmology-only would mean asking the ICB to fragment its A&G estate - a hard ask without overwhelming clinical pull.

EyeV

Profile. Eye-care-specific optometry-to-ophthalmology referral platform. Narrower scope than Cinapsis, but more directly competitive with Lutra's core. Often adopted as an ICB's specific Single Point of Access for community optometry referrals into hospital ophthalmology.

Footprint. Live as the SPoA in a number of NHS England ICBs covering community-optometry referrals into hospital eye services. EyeV's lighter functional footprint relative to Lutra has historically been part of its appeal - it is easier to roll in quickly, though without the outcomes-data and post-op-pathway capabilities Lutra offers.

Implication for Lutra. EyeV is the most direct head-to-head we encounter at the ICB SPoA level. Where it is already in place, the procurement question is no longer whether to adopt a digital optometry referral system - that question has been answered - but whether to switch.

Caveat - specific tender awards to be verified: a definitive list of NHS England ICB tenders awarded to Cinapsis and EyeV is not reproduced here. Specific award notices, ICBs and date windows should be cross-referenced against Contracts Finder and Find a Tender Service before this section is used externally. The general positioning above (multi-ICB Cinapsis A&G coverage; multiple-ICB EyeV SPoA coverage) is well established; the precise list of ICBs and award values requires verification.
What this evidence demonstrates

Two competitor categories, both closing doors before product fit is the question.

PES at the commissioning layer and Cinapsis & EyeV at the SPoA layer together account for a substantial portion of the NHS England addressable market for community-optometry-to-ophthalmology referrals. Combined with the national-programme commitments documented in Scotland (Appendix B), the EPR-rollout freeze pattern documented in Somerset (Appendix C), and the multi-stakeholder coordination cost documented in Devon (Appendix D), the picture is consistent: the doors that are closed in the NHS England market are not closed because of Lutra's product. They are closed because of decisions taken by other parties before Lutra arrives in the room.

Appendix F · Status

Private market - integration status & commercial work in flight.

Where Lutra Connect stands across the optometry-side ecosystem (PMS and diagnostic equipment partners) and the ophthalmology-side EPR/CRM platforms it integrates into, plus the work currently outstanding on financial projections for the optometry and private-ophthalmology markets.

Optometry-side - PMS & diagnostic ecosystem

Ocuco / Acuitas 3 - partnership signed

A partnership agreement has been signed with Ocuco for Lutra Connect to be available as a plugin on the Ocuco marketplace for Acuitas 3, Ocuco's market-leading practice management system.

Acuitas 3 is being deployed across Ocuco's enterprise client base - including Asda - which gives Lutra Connect a credible distribution route into a large segment of UK optometry without needing each practice group to procure separately.

This addresses the double-keying objection that has historically been a sticking point with optometrists: with the Acuitas 3 plugin live, optometrists can launch Lutra Connect from within the system they already use day-to-day.

Topcon Harmony - talks in progress

Initial conversations have begun with Topcon on integrating Lutra Connect with Topcon Harmony - the data-management platform used alongside Topcon's diagnostic imaging equipment in optometry practices.

Discussions also include Lutra joining the Topcon Innovation Accelerator, which would give us a structured route into Topcon's developer ecosystem and broader visibility with Topcon-equipped practices.

Early stage; no formal agreement yet. Strategic value sits in being directly downstream of OCT and other diagnostic data captured in the optometry chair.

Ophthalmology-side - EPR / CRM

Semble - live integration

The integration with Semble is live. Semble is the most widely used EPR among private ophthalmologists and consulting groups, and the integration enables Lutra Connect to act as the optometry-facing front end into existing Semble-based practices without requiring them to change their underlying system.

This is the route by which optometrist-driven referrals, advice & guidance, and Rapid Consultant Review can flow directly into a private clinic's existing patient record.

Carebit - integration in scoping

Movement with Carebit has been slower. The principal technical constraint is that Carebit does not currently expose an open API, which materially raises the cost and complexity of integration relative to Semble.

Carebit is used by a number of high-value private surgical providers, so the integration remains strategically important - but the timeline is dependent on either Carebit's API roadmap or a bilateral integration arrangement.
